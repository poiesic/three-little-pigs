# AI Writing Tropes to Avoid

Patterns that mark prose as machine-generated. Any single instance might be
a deliberate choice. The problem is density -- when several appear together or
one recurs throughout a manuscript, the prose reads as synthetic.

Adapted from [tropes.fyi](https://tropes.fyi) with additions for fiction.

---

## Prose Mechanics

### Metronomic Sentence Length

AI prose settles into a rhythm where most sentences land between 12 and 20
words. Paragraph after paragraph at the same cadence. Human writers
naturally produce much wider variation -- a 4-word sentence followed by a
40-word sentence followed by a 15-word sentence. When every sentence hums
at the same frequency, the prose feels frictionless in a bad way.

### Filter Words in Close POV

"He saw," "she noticed," "he felt," "she heard," "he realized," "she
thought." In close third or first person, the reader is already inside the
character's head. These verbs insert a pane of glass between the character
and the experience. Just show what they saw, heard, or felt.

**Instead of:** "She noticed the light had changed to a sickly yellow."
**Write:** "The light had gone a sickly yellow."

### Instant Emotional Resolution

AI introduces emotional tension and resolves it within the same paragraph
or the next sentence. Real emotional states persist, complicate other
actions, and resolve messily over time. When a character is furious in one
sentence and understanding in the next, it reads as a model satisfying a
narrative arc at the smallest possible scale.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "She was furious. But then she understood why he'd done it."
- "His chest tightened with grief. He took a breath and let it go."
- "The betrayal stung. Still, she could see his side of things."

### The Temperature Emotion

Describing emotions as temperatures or movements through the body in
generic terms. This is one of the strongest Claude-specific tells. Real
physical descriptions of emotion are specific to the character and the
moment. A knot in the gut, a jaw clenching, fingernails cutting into a
palm -- these are concrete. "Something cold" or "warmth spreading" are
vending-machine emotions.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "Something cold moved through her chest."
- "A warmth spread through him at the words."
- "Ice settled in his stomach."
- "Heat bloomed behind her eyes."

### Hedging Verbs

"Seemed," "appeared," "as if," "almost," "somehow." AI uses these to
soften descriptions, creating a permanent haze over the narrative. In close
POV, the character either perceives something or they don't. Occasional
uncertainty is realistic. Constant hedging is a model avoiding commitment.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "He seemed tired" (when the POV character can see him clearly)
- "The room appeared empty" (just say it's empty or describe what makes it
  seem empty)
- "She somehow knew he was lying."

### Participial Phrase Stacking

Opening sentences with "-ing" phrases, especially multiple per paragraph.
AI uses participial openers to vary sentence structure, but overuse creates
a distinctive lurching rhythm where every sentence starts with an action
happening simultaneously with the main clause.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "Crossing the room, she picked up the glass. Turning it in her hands,
  she noticed the crack. Sighing, she set it down."

### The Articulate Character

Every character speaks in complete, well-formed thoughts with perfect
vocabulary and self-awareness. Real people trail off, use the wrong word,
talk around what they mean, and rarely articulate their emotions with
precision. AI dialogue tends toward a baseline articulateness that
flattens all characters into the same register.

### Dead-Weight Modifiers

"Very," "really," "just," "quite," "somewhat," "rather," "slightly,"
"a bit." These weaken whatever they modify. Cut them unless they're doing
specific character work in dialogue (a character who hedges habitually, for
instance).

### Weak Verb + Adverb

"Walked slowly" instead of "trudged." "Said quietly" instead of
"murmured." "Looked angrily" instead of "glared." The adverb is almost
always a sign that the verb isn't doing its job. Find the verb that carries
the meaning on its own.

### Redundant Anatomy

"Nodded his head," "shrugged her shoulders," "blinked his eyes," "clenched
his fists," "sat down," "stood up." The body part or direction is implied
by the verb.

## Sentence Structure

### Negative Parallelism

"It's not X -- it's Y." The single most common AI writing tell. Used to
create false profundity by framing ordinary observations as surprising
reframes. Once per piece can be effective. Multiple instances are a
fingerprint.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "It wasn't a house. It was a tomb."
- "She didn't walk -- she prowled."
- "This wasn't grief. It was something older."

### "Not X. Not Y. Just Z."

The dramatic countdown. Negating two or more things before revealing the
actual point. Creates manufactured suspense around information that doesn't
warrant it.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "Not anger. Not sadness. Just a hollow quiet."
- "Not the door. Not the window. The mirror."

### The Self-Answered Question

"The result? Devastating." A rhetorical question the narrator poses and
immediately answers for dramatic effect. This is a nonfiction tic that
bleeds into AI fiction, especially in interior monologue.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "The worst part? She'd known all along."
- "The irony? He'd built the cage himself."

### Anaphora Abuse

Repeating the same sentence opening three or more times in succession.
Once or twice is rhetoric. Three or more is a pattern the model can't
break out of.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "She remembered the way he laughed. She remembered the kitchen light.
  She remembered his hands."
- "There was the smell of pine. There was the creak of the porch. There
  was the silence after."

### Tricolon Abuse

Rule-of-three constructions stacked back to back. A single tricolon lands.
Multiple tricolons in sequence reveal a model reaching for the same
structural trick repeatedly.

### Gerund Fragment Litany

A claim followed by a stream of verbless "-ing" fragments as standalone
sentences. The fragments add rhythm without content.

**Avoid patterns like:**
- "She kept busy. Cleaning the kitchen. Sorting the mail. Watering plants
  that were already dead."

(Note: this can be a legitimate stylistic choice when used sparingly for
a specific effect. The problem is frequency.)

## Narrative Patterns

### The Mirror Moment

Characters catching sight of themselves in a reflective surface so the
author can deliver a physical description. One of the oldest writing
workshop clichés, and AI reaches for it constantly.

### Sensory Cataloging

Describing a new space by mechanically listing what each sense perceives:
"The room smelled of cedar. Dust motes floated in the light. Somewhere, a
clock ticked." AI processes environments as checklists. Human writers
select the one or two details that matter and let the rest fill in.

### The Meaningful Pause

Characters who "paused" or sat in "a long silence" or experienced
"a beat" before speaking, especially when delivering important lines.
Occasional pauses are natural. When every significant line of dialogue is
preceded by a pause, it's a model signaling importance rather than
trusting the dialogue to carry its own weight.

### Stage Direction Choreography

Excessive tracking of physical movement through a scene: standing,
crossing, sitting, turning, leaning. Characters rearranging themselves
constantly between lines of dialogue. Some blocking is necessary. A play-
by-play of every shift in posture is a model filling space.

### The Knowing Glance

Characters who communicate complex meaning through eye contact, shared
looks, or small gestures that somehow convey specific, articulable
thoughts. "Their eyes met, and she understood." What did she understand?
If you can't specify it without the glance, the glance isn't doing
the work.

### Weather as Emotion

Rain when characters are sad. Sun breaking through when hope arrives.
Storm gathering when conflict builds. Pathetic fallacy used once can work.
Used as a system -- every scene's weather mirroring its emotional content
-- is a model leaning on the simplest possible environmental storytelling.

### The Paragraph-Final Reveal

Burying the important information or emotional beat at the end of a
paragraph, often after a line break or in a short final sentence. AI
structures paragraphs like jokes -- setup, setup, punchline. When every
paragraph ends with its most dramatic moment, the structure becomes
predictable.

## Dialogue

### Attribution Verbs

"Exclaimed," "retorted," "queried," "breathed," "intoned," "mused."
In literary fiction, "said" and "asked" handle the vast majority of
attribution. Action beats ("She set down her glass.") replace tags when
possible. Creative attribution verbs draw attention to themselves and away
from the dialogue.

### Adverbs on Attribution

"Said quietly," "said angrily," "asked hesitantly." The dialogue itself
should convey the tone. If it doesn't, the fix is rewriting the dialogue,
not adding an adverb.

### Monologue Speeches

Characters who deliver multi-sentence, perfectly structured arguments in
conversation. Real speech is fragmented, interrupted, and recursive.
People circle back, lose their thread, get cut off. AI characters tend to
deliver complete paragraphs that read like written essays spoken aloud.

### Identical Register

All characters use the same vocabulary level, sentence complexity, and
rhetorical patterns. A teenager and a professor should not sound the same.
Neither should a nervous character and a confident one. AI flattens these
differences unless the character profile strongly constrains the output.

## Word Choice

### "Quietly" and Other Magic Adverbs

Overuse of "quietly" to convey understated power or significance. AI
reaches for this adverb to make mundane descriptions feel important.
Also: "deeply," "fundamentally," "utterly."

### "Delve" and Friends

The classic AI vocabulary set: "delve," "utilize," "leverage" (as a verb),
"robust," "streamline," "harness," "myriad," "pivotal," "multifaceted."
These words aren't wrong, but their density in AI text is far higher than
in human writing.

### The "Serves As" Dodge

Replacing simple "is" with pompous alternatives: "serves as," "stands as,"
"represents," "marks." AI avoids basic copulas because repetition penalties
push it toward fancier constructions. In fiction, this shows up in
narration: "The building served as a reminder" instead of "The building
reminded her."

## Formatting

### Em-Dash Addiction

Two or three em dashes in a piece is normal. Twenty is a model using them
as a universal connector -- for parenthetical asides, dramatic pauses,
pivot points, and list separators.

### Unicode Decoration

Curly quotes, Unicode arrows, and special characters that a human typing
in a text editor wouldn't produce. Straight quotes and `--` for em dashes
are what writers actually type. The build pipeline handles conversion.

## Composition

### One-Feeling Scenes

Every scene has exactly one emotional note that every element reinforces.
The character feels one thing, the environment reflects it, the dialogue
confirms it. Real scenes contain competing emotions, contradictions, and
moments where the environment is indifferent to the character's state.

### The Thematic Bow

Endings that circle back to the opening image or phrase, tying the
narrative into a neat loop. AI has a strong bias toward circular structure
because it reads as "satisfying" in training data. Sometimes a story
should end somewhere different from where it started, without winking at
the beginning.

### Content Duplication

The same beat, image, or observation appearing twice in a scene or across
adjacent scenes. The model loses track of what it has already established,
especially in longer outputs. A dead giveaway of unedited AI text.

### Fractal Summaries

Summarizing what just happened at the end of a scene or chapter. The
reader was there. They don't need a recap. AI does this because its
training includes texts with explicit conclusions, but fiction earns its
endings through implication, not restatement.

---

Remember: any of these used once might be a deliberate choice. The
problem is density. Write like a human: varied, imperfect, specific,
and willing to leave things unresolved.
