Susan was repointing the mortar on her south wall when she saw her brothers coming up the road. Brad was in sandals and covered in straw. Terence was clutching a bundle of twigs wearing the expression of a man whose dog had just died.

She set down her trowel.

"Kevin?" she said when they got close enough.

"Kevin," they said together.

She'd been expecting this since April, when Kevin posted a video of himself benchpressing a log on social media with the caption "DANA WHITE CALL ME." She'd actually reinforced the south wall that week. Double-thick mortar joints. Rebar on twelve-inch centers. She'd wanted to tell her brothers to do the same, but Brad had been busy giving his TEDx talk and Terence had been sourcing birch from Vermont, and neither of them listened to her about anything structural, ever, not once, not in thirty-eight years.

"He's filming an audition tape," Brad said. "For the UFC."

"I know. Sit down. Don't touch anything."

She put them in the kitchen with water and went back outside to finish the mortar. If Kevin was following them, she had maybe an hour. The east wall was solid. The whole house was solid. Twelve-inch brick walls on a poured concrete foundation, through-bolted to a steel frame she'd welded herself. She'd built it to residential code and then exceeded that code by a factor she didn't disclose to her brothers because they'd call her paranoid.

They'd be right. She was paranoid. She was also the only one of the three of them who still had a house.

The tripod arrived forty-five minutes later.

Susan poured herself a coffee and sat by the front window to watch. Kevin was setting up in the yard. New tank top. This one was hand-lettered and said "100% LUPINE" across the front. He'd added wristbands. He was bouncing on his toes like a boxer before a bout, throwing combinations at the air.

He hit record.

"Dana. My man. You've seen the straw. You've seen the sticks. Now I'm going after brick." He cracked his neck both directions. "This is the big one. The final boss. After this, there is nothing left to prove. You are looking at the most dangerous set of lungs in the animal kingdom."

Susan sipped her coffee.

Kevin took his stance. Wider than usual. He shook out his arms. Rolled his shoulders. Drew in the biggest breath Susan had ever seen a mammal take. His chest expanded until the tank top seams audibly strained.

He blew.

The curtains moved. The house didn't. A leaf skittered across the front step.

Kevin stared at the house. The house stared back.

"Cut." He walked to the camera and hit a button. Stood there a moment, hands on hips. Walked back to his mark.

"Take two, Dana. That was a warm-up. You want to see the real thing? Here's the real thing."

Bigger breath. Face going red under the fur. He blew so hard his ears pinned back and his tail went rigid.

The leaf skittered back the other direction.

Behind Susan, Brad whispered, "Bro."

Terence said, "The mortar joints aren't even vibrating."

They weren't. Susan had mixed that mortar herself. Portland cement, lime, and sand in a ratio she'd calculated for compressive strength, not aesthetics. She hadn't blogged about it. She hadn't given a talk about it. She'd just done the math and mixed the batch.

Outside, Kevin was bent over with his hands on his knees. He straightened up and addressed the camera again.

"Okay. Full transparency, Dana. I've been overtraining. Three houses in one day. That's an insane workload. Any scout would tell you that's too much volume for a single session. I should have periodized this."

He tried again. Nothing.

"Also, and I want to be real with you here, Dana. This house is overbuilt. Look at this." He walked up and knocked on the wall with his fist. Then shook his hand because brick is hard. "This is not standard residential construction. She's got rebar in here. This is a bunker. Nobody builds like this. You can't judge my lung output against a structure that's clearly been over-engineered for the neighborhood."

He blew again. His face went purple. A vein stood out on his forehead that Susan hadn't previously known existed.

The house didn't move.

"I'm also cutting weight right now," Kevin told the camera. He was breathing hard. "Down about eight pounds this cycle. That affects your oxygen capacity. Everybody knows that. So what you're seeing here is maybe seventy percent of my actual output. Seventy-five, tops."

He gave it one more shot with the lungs. Planted his feet, arched his back, filled his chest until he looked like a furry balloon, and blew with a sound like a jet engine at idle.

A shingle on the garden shed shifted slightly. The house did nothing.

Kevin stood there for a long moment. Then he addressed the camera. "Change of strategy, Dana. What you're about to see is my ground game. Totally lethal."

He charged the house. Full sprint across the yard, dropped his shoulder, and attempted a double-leg takedown on the front wall. He wrapped both arms around the corner of the building and drove with his legs, grunting, his feet churning divots in the lawn.

The wall did not move. Kevin slid down the brickwork and landed on his back. He got up, shook it off, and tried a rear naked choke on the drainpipe. The drainpipe held. He transitioned to a guillotine on the windowsill, which went about as well as putting a headlock on a table.

Behind Susan, Brad said, "Is he wrestling your house?"

"Yes," Susan said.

Kevin was now trying to get the front step into an armbar. He had one leg over the concrete and was pulling with both hands, his face beet red, narrating through clenched teeth. "This is a submission hold, Dana. Once I get the leverage, it's over."

The front step did not submit.

"Should we call someone?" Terence said.

"Like who?" Susan said.

"I don't know. Animal control. A therapist."

"Bro needs help," Brad said. "His aura is definitely mid."

Kevin sat down in the yard. He sat there for a while, staring at the camera, which was still recording. His "100% LUPINE" tank top was soaked through with sweat. One of the wristbands had fallen off.

"I'm gonna re-edit the reel," he said to no one. "Use just the first two. Straw was the money shot. Slow-mo would be epic."

He packed up the tripod. It took him three tries because his arms were shaking. He walked off down the road, muttering about periodization and altitude training.

Susan finished her coffee.

Brad came up behind her. "Bro, your house is, like, aggressively sturdy."

"It's brick," she said.

Terence appeared at her other shoulder. "The foundation work. Is that a continuous pour or segmented?"

"Continuous."

"And the rebar spacing?"

"Twelve-inch centers."

"That's very tight."

"Yes."

The three of them stood at the window and watched Kevin disappear down the road, tripod over one shoulder.

Susan picked up her trowel. The south wall still needed two more rows of repointing, and she wanted to finish before dark.
