Terence was arranging his latest acquisition, a white birch expression with a near-perfect 40-degree fork and what he considered a profoundly emotional grain pattern, in the display case by the front door when Brad walked in without knocking. He was covered in straw and breathing hard, which Terence found rude on both counts.

"Bro. Bro. Terence. Bro."

"You're getting straw on my reclaimed pine floors."

"Kevin blew my house down." Brad bent over with his hands on his knees to catch his breath. "He's got a camera. He's making a video for Dana White."

"Dana White."

"The UFC guy, bro."

Terence knew who Dana White was. He'd simply hoped that repeating the name would make the sentence make more sense. It didn't. "Kevin destroyed your house for a video?"

"An audition tape. He wants to fight MMA professionally." Brad straightened up and looked around Terence's living room for the first time. "Dude, why do you have so many sticks?"

Terence closed his eyes for a moment. "They're not sticks, Brad. They're expressions." He gestured at the sugar maple on the shelf. "That one took three seasons to develop its patina. I have a certificate of provenance for it." He adjusted it a quarter inch to the left. "And this house is built from architectural-grade timber curated from six different old-growth forests. Each beam was selected for grain density, tonal resonance, and emotional character. It's not going anywhere."

Brad left a trail of straw as he walked to the couch. Terence grabbed the broom.

"You're telling me," Terence said, sweeping around Brad's feet, "that Kevin the Wolf blew down your straw house."

"One breath, bro. One breath."

"Because straw is a garbage building material."

"Straw is renewable and carbon-negative and I gave a TEDx talk about it."

"At the community center. To eleven people."

"Fourteen."

Terence leaned the broom against the wall and looked at his own walls. Solid. Interlocking tongue-and-groove joinery he'd designed himself. White oak posts. Cedar shingle roof. Every timber hand-selected for grain density, structural integrity, and what Terence described in his Substack as "narrative coherence." The post about the build process had seven subscribers, which was traction.

"He can't blow this down," Terence said.

The tripod scraped up the front path about twenty minutes later.

Terence watched from the window as Kevin set up his equipment in the yard. He'd changed tank tops. This one said "WOLF MODE" in iron-on letters, one of which was already peeling. He checked his hair in the camera's flip screen, then hit record.

"Dana. Kevin Wolf. Round two." He shadowboxed. Left, left, right. The right was slow and Terence could see him wince. Probably a shoulder thing, but Kevin played it off as a dramatic pause. "You saw what I did to the straw house. Child's play. Warm-up round. Now I'm stepping up to timber. That's right. Wood. Hard. Wood." He paused, apparently pleased with himself. "Let's see how it holds up against these lungs."

Kevin turned toward the house and took his wide stance. Chest out. Deep breath.

Behind Terence, Brad said "Bro" very quietly.

Kevin blew.

The front wall held for about a second and a half, which Terence thought was respectable. Then the tongue-and-groove joints started popping like knuckles, one after another in a line, and the wall buckled outward and came apart in long planks. The cedar shingles went next, lifting off the roof in sheets. The display case by the door tipped forward and shattered, and the white birch expression with the profoundly emotional grain pattern rolled into a puddle.

Terence watched it all happen from inside the collapsing structure, which he recognized was not the safest place to process grief, but he couldn't look away from his walls separating at the joints. He'd fitted those joints himself. He'd written about those joints. They'd had narrative coherence.

Brad pulled him out by the arm.

Kevin was already at the camera. "TWO FOR TWO, DANA! Two for two! Different materials, same result. You can't train this. You're born with this. Big Bad, baby. BIG BAD."

He ripped off the peeling tank top and flexed. Terence was fairly sure one of the iron-on letters was stuck to Kevin's back fur.

"We gotta go to Susan's," Brad said.

Terence looked at the wreckage. His sugar maple expression was under a pile of cedar shingles. The certificate of provenance was gone.

"Yeah," he said. "Let's go to Susan's."
